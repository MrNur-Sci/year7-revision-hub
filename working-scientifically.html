Big Idea

Scientists plan investigations so results are reliable and conclusions are valid. Every choice — what to measure, what to keep the same, how many repeats — affects how much we can trust the data.

Section 1

Lab Safety & the Bunsen Burner

Before any practical, scientists identify hazards and reduce risks.

hazard = something that could cause harm risk = the chance of harm & how serious precaution = action that reduces risk
⚠️ Hazard vs Risk vs Precaution — all three are different!

The Bunsen flame is the hazard. The risk is that loose hair could catch fire. The precaution is to tie hair back. Students often confuse hazard and risk — they are not the same thing.

🔥 Before lighting

Goggles on · Hair tied back · Bag off floor · Air hole closed · Check gas tube is secure

🔵 The two flames

Yellow = safety flame (air hole closed, cooler)
Blue = roaring flame (air hole open, hotter — used for heating)

🧯 After use

Close air hole before turning off gas · Never leave flame unattended · Wait for equipment to cool

Section 2

Variables and Fair Testing

Every investigation has three types of variable. Getting these right makes your test fair and your conclusions valid.

independent variable = what you change dependent variable = what you measure control variable = what you keep the same
⚠️ Control variable ≠ Control experiment

A control variable is a factor you keep the same to make the test fair. It is not the same as a "control experiment." At Year 7, control variable = things kept the same.

Hypothesis structure — always use this:
"If I [change the independent variable], then [the dependent variable] will [increase/decrease] because [scientific reason]."
Worked Example

Investigation: How does ramp height affect the speed of a toy car?

Independent variable: Height of the ramp (cm)

Dependent variable: Speed of the car (m/s)

Control variables: Same car, same ramp, same distance, same surface

Hypothesis: "If I increase the ramp height, then the car speed will increase, because a higher ramp gives more gravitational potential energy."

Section 3

Tables, Means and Anomalies

Good data tables, calculated means, and spotting anomalies are all essential skills.

repeat = doing the same measurement more than once mean = sum of values ÷ number of values anomaly = a result that doesn't fit the pattern reliable = results that are consistent when repeated
Worked Example — Calculating a Mean & Spotting an Anomaly

Five repeat times for sugar dissolving (s): 18, 20, 19, 47, 21

Step 1 — Spot the anomaly: 47 s doesn't fit the pattern (others are ~18–21 s). Exclude it.

Step 2 — Mean of remaining values: (18 + 20 + 19 + 21) ÷ 4 = 78 ÷ 4 = 19.5 s

Section 5

Conclusions and Evaluations

A conclusion uses data to answer the question. An evaluation judges the quality of the method.

⚠️ Conclusion without data = weak conclusion

Weak: "Height affects speed." Strong: "As height increased from 5 cm to 25 cm, speed increased from 1.2 to 4.9 m/s — a positive correlation supporting my hypothesis."

Worked Example — Dissolving Sugars

Strong conclusion: "As sugar mass increased from 5 g to 15 g, dissolving time increased from 12 s to 36 s — a positive correlation supporting my hypothesis."

Strong evaluation: "I repeated each measurement three times and calculated a mean. One anomaly at 15 g (58 s) was excluded. To improve reliability I could take five repeats per mass."

Write a conclusion

Investigation: ramp height → car speed. Results: 5 cm→1.2 m/s, 10 cm→2.5 m/s, 15 cm→3.6 m/s, 20 cm→4.8 m/s.
