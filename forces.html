Big Idea

Forces cause objects to start, stop, speed up, slow down or change direction. When forces are balanced, motion stays constant. When unbalanced, objects accelerate — described by F = ma.

Section 1

Speed

Speed is a scalar quantity — it tells us how much distance is covered per second, with no direction needed.

v = d ÷ tv = speed (m/s)  |  d = distance (m)  |  t = time (s)   → rearranges to: d = v×t   and   t = d÷v
speed (v) = m/s or km/h scalar = no direction velocity = speed WITH direction (vector)
Worked Example

Q: A runner travels 200 m in 25 s. Find speed.

Formula: v = d ÷ t  →  v = 200 ÷ 25  →  v = 8 m/s

Section 2

Distance-Time Graphs

A steeper gradient = faster speed. A horizontal line = stopped. The gradient equals the speed.

⚠️ Gradient = speed on a D-T graph

Gradient = rise ÷ run = Δdistance ÷ Δtime = speed. A steeper section doesn't mean "accelerating" on a D-T graph — it means a higher constant speed.

Section 3 · Simulation

Speed-Time Graphs and Acceleration

Speed-time graphs are completely different from distance-time graphs. The gradient = acceleration. The area under the graph = distance.

⚠️ Don't confuse D-T and S-T graphs!

On a D-T graph: horizontal = stopped. On a S-T graph: horizontal = constant speed (still moving!). This is one of the most common exam errors.

a = Δv ÷ ta = acceleration (m/s²)  |  Δv = change in speed (m/s)  |  t = time (s)

Forces, Resultants and F = ma

A resultant force is the single force that has the same effect as all forces combined. If it's zero, motion stays constant. If non-zero, acceleration occurs.

⚠️ Balanced forces does NOT mean stopped!

Balanced forces (resultant = 0 N) means constant velocity — the object could be stationary OR moving at constant speed. You cannot tell which without more information.

F = m × aF = force (N)  |  m = mass (kg)  |  a = acceleration (m/s²)   → a = F÷m  |  m = F÷a

Section 5 · Simulation

Gravity, Weight and Falling Objects

Weight is a force caused by gravity. Mass and weight are different! As objects fall, air resistance grows until forces balance — terminal velocity.

W = m × gW = weight (N)  |  m = mass (kg)  |  g = gravitational field strength (N/kg)
⚠️ Mass ≠ Weight — the most common physics error at GCSE!

Mass (kg) = amount of matter — same everywhere in the universe. Weight (N) = force of gravity on that mass — different on every planet. A 10 kg bag has mass 10 kg everywhere, but weight 100 N on Earth, 16 N on the Moon.
