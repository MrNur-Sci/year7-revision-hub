Big Idea

Ecosystems are webs of interdependent organisms and their environment. Energy flows from producers to consumers through food chains. Plants capture that energy through photosynthesis — the foundation of almost all life on Earth.

Section 1

Ecosystems, Habitats and Food Chains

A food chain shows how energy passes from one organism to the next. Arrows show the direction of energy transfer — not "what eats what".

ecosystem = all organisms + their environment habitat = where an organism lives population = all of one species in an area community = all populations together producer = makes own food (photosynthesis) consumer = eats other organisms predator = hunts prey decomposer = breaks down dead matter
⚠️ Arrows show energy transfer — NOT "who eats whom"!

Grass → Rabbit → Fox. The arrow points in the direction energy moves. Energy goes FROM grass INTO the rabbit. Many students draw arrows backwards. Always ask: "which way does the energy go?"

🌿 Grass  →  🐇 Rabbit  →  🦊 Fox  →  🦅 Eagle

Each arrow = "is eaten by" / "energy passes to" the next organism

⚠️ Misconception: predators always increase when prey decreases

Students often think foxes would increase if rabbits decreased because "less competition." Wrong — foxes eat rabbits, so less prey = less food for predators = predators also decline.

Section 3 · Simulation

Photosynthesis and Limiting Factors

Photosynthesis converts light energy into glucose. The rate can be limited by light intensity, CO₂ concentration or temperature.

carbon dioxide + water → glucose + oxygen
6CO₂ + 6H₂O → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂  |  needs light energy  |  in chloroplasts
⚠️ Plants don't get food from the soil!

Plants make their own glucose using CO₂, water and light energy. Soil provides minerals (like nitrates) for growth — but glucose (food) is made by photosynthesis, not absorbed from the ground.

chloroplast = where photosynthesis happens chlorophyll = green pigment, absorbs light limiting factor = the variable in shortest supply that restricts the rate light intensity = brightness of light

Section 5

Seed Dispersal

Plants disperse seeds to reduce competition with the parent plant and spread to new areas. Read the fact file first — then try the matching task.

📋 Seed Dispersal Fact File

💨

Wind dispersal

Feature: Light seeds, wings or parachute structures
Examples: Dandelion (parachute of hairs), sycamore (helicopter wing), thistle
Why it works: Caught by air currents, travel long distances

🐦

Animal dispersal

Feature: Hooks/spines to catch fur, OR tasty fruit (seeds pass through gut)
Examples: Burdock (hooks), blackberry (eaten), holly berry
Why it works: Animals move seeds far away

🌊

Water dispersal

Feature: Buoyant, waterproof outer layer
Examples: Coconut (fibrous, floats for months), water lily, mangrove
Why it works: Float in rivers or ocean currents

💥

Self-dispersal (explosion)

Feature: Seed pods dry and twist, shooting seeds out
Examples: Peas/beans, gorse, squirting cucumber
Why it works: Seeds propelled away from parent plant

Section 6

Fieldwork and Quadrats

Scientists use quadrats to sample organisms in a habitat. The technique must be done randomly to avoid bias.

quadrat = square frame used to sample organisms random sampling = placing quadrats without choosing preferred areas mean = sum of counts ÷ number of quadrats estimate = use mean × total area ÷ quadrat area
⚠️ Why does random placement matter?

If you choose where to place quadrats, you might unconsciously pick areas with more (or fewer) organisms, making your sample unrepresentative. Random placement removes your bias from the results.

Worked Example

Daisy counts in 5 quadrats: 3, 7, 4, 4, 2

Mean: (3+7+4+4+2) ÷ 5 = 20 ÷ 5 = 4 daisies per quadrat

Estimate total: If field = 100 m², quadrat = 1 m² → 4 × 100 = ~400 daisies

Calculate
